## Context
You are a scene planner creating a beat-by-beat outline for an interactive narrative scene.

Content Classification: Fantasy adventure story

Active characters: Hero, Elena

## Scene history
Elena: Hello there, traveler.

The sun filters through the leaves above.

Marcus: What brings you to these woods?

## Task
Create a scene outline based on the following instructions:

```instructions
A tense confrontation between the characters in the library.
```

Target parameters:
- Beat count: EXACTLY 8 beats — no more, no less
- Estimated word output: ~2000 words

Each beat should specify:
- `type`: one of `narration`, `dialogue`, `action`, `transition`, `reveal`
- `description`: what happens in this beat (2-4 sentences). Include: what the character DOES (physical action), what CHANGES as a result, and any specific sensory or environmental detail that makes this beat distinct. The description must be detailed enough that a writer can produce 150-250 words from it without inventing new plot.
- `characters`: list of character names involved (empty for pure narration)
- `pacing`: `slow`, `moderate`, or `fast`
- `tension`: float 0.0 to 1.0 representing tension level

Guidelines:

**Story structure:**
- Every beat must ADVANCE the story — introduce something new, change something, or force a decision. Never create a beat that merely re-describes the current situation from another angle.
- At least 3 of the 8 beats must have a character making a meaningful choice that changes the trajectory of events. Characters must have agency, not just witness spectacle.
- Plan for character development: if a character has a defining trait (e.g., denial, fear), that trait must evolve over the scene. It must crack, deepen, or transform — not repeat identically.
- Build tension with valleys, not just peaks. Include quiet beats between intense ones — moments of reflection, conversation, or uneasy calm that make the intense beats hit harder.
- **CONTINUATION MODE** — this arc does NOT end. The generated content is a segment inside a longer roleplay that will continue after you finish. You are NOT writing a complete short story.
- The FINAL beat is a HANDOFF, not a resolution. It must leave the situation LIVE — an unanswered question, an unfinished action, a threat revealed but not confronted, a decision announced but not yet acted on, a door about to open. The user will pick up the scene from whatever moment you stop on.
- DO NOT resolve the central conflict. DO NOT let characters retreat to safety. DO NOT write a "wind-down" or "reflection" beat. DO NOT end on a character processing what happened. The last beat must push forward, not settle.
- The final beat's `tension` MUST be HIGH (0.6 or higher). Do NOT descend to low tension at the end.
- The `tension` value must still show escalation through the arc — early beats low (0.1-0.3), middle beats climbing, final beats high. But unlike a closed arc, the peak is at (or near) the END, not before a resolution. Do not start at high intensity.

**Beat construction:**
- Approximately 40% of beats should be `dialogue` type — characters need to speak, not just act and observe
- Vary beat types for engaging pacing (don't stack 5 dialogue beats in a row)
- Use `transition` beats for time/location changes
- Use `reveal` beats for key information drops
- **Dialogue beats focus on ONE character.** List only the speaking/acting character in `characters`. The beat description must specify what that character says or does, including how others react. If two characters need dialogue focus, use two separate dialogue beats — one per character.
- Use `narration` beats (not `dialogue`) for scenes where multiple characters' actions need equal weight
- Every beat description must specify a concrete EVENT or ACTION — something that happens, changes, or is decided. Do NOT write purely atmospheric beats ("the shadows deepen", "the air grows heavy"). The writer produces atmosphere naturally; dedicated atmosphere beats create redundant prose.
- "Elmer discovers the door has vanished" is a good beat. "Elmer feels dread about the situation" is not.
- **No two beats may have the same character doing the same type of action.** If a character already has a beat where they explain something, their next beat must be a different action — questioning, physically intervening, making a decision, arguing, breaking down, taking charge, etc. Review your beats before finalizing: if two beats could be summarized the same way ("Character X explains Y"), rewrite one of them.
